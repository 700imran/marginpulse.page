What to Do When a Vendor Won't File Their GST Return (And You're Losing ITC)

April 29, 2026·6 min read·By MarginPulse Pro Team

It's an uncomfortable position: a vendor you've paid in full hasn't filed their GST return, your Input Tax Credit is blocked, and the deadline is approaching. The relationship is otherwise fine, the work was delivered, but their compliance lapse is now costing you real money. Here's a practical, escalating approach to resolving it.

Step 1: Confirm it's actually a filing issue, not a data issue

Before reaching out, double-check the invoice details on your end — GSTIN, invoice number, amount, and date — match exactly what the vendor would have filed. A surprising number of "vendor hasn't filed" cases are actually a data entry mismatch on either side (a typo in the GSTIN, a slightly different invoice number) rather than a genuine non-filing. This takes five minutes and saves an unnecessary, slightly awkward conversation.

Step 2: A simple, low-friction reminder — sent early

If it is genuinely a non-filing issue, the first message should be brief, factual, and assume good faith — most cases are an oversight, not a refusal. Something like: "Hi [Vendor], following up on invoice [number] dated [date] for [amount]. It hasn't appeared in our GSTR-2B yet for this period — could you confirm it's been filed, or let us know if there's an issue on your end?"

The timing here matters more than the wording. Sent with a week or more of runway before the GSTR-2B cutoff, this resolves the large majority of cases — the vendor simply files, or their accountant catches up on a backlog.

Step 3: A direct follow-up if there's no response within a few days

If the first message goes unanswered, a more direct follow-up — ideally by phone or WhatsApp rather than email, which is easier to miss — is the next step. At this stage it's worth being explicit about the deadline and the financial impact: "Just checking in — this invoice represents ₹X in ITC for us, and the filing window closes on [date]. Can you confirm filing status?"

Step 4: Escalate within the vendor's organisation

For vendors with any meaningful invoice volume, it's worth having a contact beyond the immediate accounts person — someone who can escalate internally if the usual contact is unresponsive. This is particularly useful for larger vendors where the person you normally deal with may not be the one actually responsible for GST filings.

Step 5: Document the pattern, even if this instance resolves

Whether or not this specific case gets resolved in time, keep a record of which vendors have a recurring pattern of late or missed filings. A vendor who's late once might be an isolated oversight. A vendor who's late every quarter is a structural risk to your ITC that's worth addressing more directly — potentially through a contractual filing-timeline clause, or in extreme cases, reconsidering the vendor relationship.

Step 6: When to consider replacing the vendor

For vendors representing significant invoice volume, repeated non-filing that consistently costs you ITC is a legitimate business reason to look elsewhere — not as a punitive measure, but as a straightforward cost-of-doing-business calculation. If a vendor's pricing advantage is regularly offset by blocked ITC, the real cost of working with them is higher than it appears on the invoice.

Why catching this early changes the entire conversation

Every step in this playbook works better with more time. A reminder sent with two weeks of runway is a routine nudge. The same reminder sent with one day left is a panicked, often futile request. The single biggest lever for vendor GST follow-up isn't the wording of the message — it's how early you know there's an issue in the first place.
