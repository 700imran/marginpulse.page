RBI Account Aggregator Explained: What It Means for Your Finance Team

June 12, 2026·6 min read·By MarginPulse Pro Team

If you've ever hesitated to connect your bank account to a finance tool because it felt like handing over your password to a stranger, you're not alone — and you're right to be cautious. But India now has a regulated framework that solves exactly this problem, and it's worth understanding before you decide whether to use it.

What is the Account Aggregator framework?

The Account Aggregator (AA) framework is a system set up under guidelines from the Reserve Bank of India that lets you share your financial data — like bank transactions — with another company, without ever giving that company your bank login credentials. Instead, you authorise the connection directly through your bank or a licensed AA app, and the data flows through a regulated, consent-based pipe.

Think of it less like handing someone your house key, and more like giving a courier a one-time, revocable pass that only lets them deliver one specific package to one specific address — and you can cancel that pass at any moment.

Why this matters more than it sounds like it does

Before AA, businesses that wanted automated bank data into a tool like an accounting platform had exactly two options: manually export and upload CSV statements every month, or use a tool that asked for net-banking credentials directly (a serious security risk, and one most banks explicitly warn against).

The AA framework removes that trade-off entirely. You get live, automated access to your transaction data, without exposing your actual banking credentials to any third party — including us.

How the connection actually works, step by step

  1. You initiate the request from within the app (e.g. MarginPulse Pro) asking to connect a specific bank account.
  2. You're redirected to your bank's AA consent screen (or a licensed AA app like the ones run by major banks), where you explicitly approve what data is shared and for how long.
  3. Data flows directly from your bank to the requesting app through the regulated AA pipeline — read-only, and only for what you approved.
  4. You can revoke access at any time from your bank's app or AA dashboard — no need to contact the company you connected to.

What "read-only" actually guarantees

This is the part that matters most for trust: a read-only AA connection cannot initiate a transfer, make a payment, or change anything in your account. It is architecturally incapable of doing so — there's no API call in the AA specification that allows a write action through this channel. The worst-case scenario of a compromised AA connection is that someone sees your transaction history, not that money moves.

What this enables for reconciliation specifically

Before AA, "automated" reconciliation tools were really "automated once you manually upload a file" tools. The actual bank data was always a snapshot from whenever someone last exported it — usually once a month, often later than that.

With a live AA connection, a reconciliation engine can see new transactions the moment they post. That changes the entire nature of the problem: instead of comparing last month's bank activity against last month's invoices in a single batch job, you can compare them continuously, and surface a mismatch the same day it happens — while it's still easy to investigate and fix.

What to ask before connecting any tool via AA

  • Does the tool request read-only access, or does it ask for broader permissions than it needs?
  • Can you revoke access independently, from your own bank app, without depending on the third party to process the request?
  • Is the company explicit about what data it pulls and how long it retains it?

Any legitimate AA-integrated platform should answer all three clearly. If a tool is vague about these, that's worth treating as a red flag regardless of how good the rest of the product looks.
